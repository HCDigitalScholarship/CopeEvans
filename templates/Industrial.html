The Industrial Age

A concept important to the success of this academic project is that during the Industrial Age relationships and roles, and subsequently values and behaviors were shaken, revised, and altered. Select examples of such relationships are people:technology, people:economy, people:politics, people:city, people:religion, and people:people. Finally, these new values and systems brought groups in contact with influences that they had not been previously in contact with.

Technology

During the Industrial Age, an explosion of novel technologies provided the proper mechanism to extend, accelerate, and intensify networks that were previously established. These technologies were integral in causing interactions that influenced how people, social groups, and society as a whole developed new values and relationships.

Philadelphia

Philadelphia will provide the exemplary microcosm to reveal the uniquely mutated society formed as a byproduct of these inventions and the larger forces driving and reacting to these rapid changes, while coincidentally allowing this project the necessary lense to intersect the Quaker religion through its historical roots and radical transformation.

New forms of transportation such as bridges,turnpikes, canals, steamboats, and railroad, were adopted by the city in the 19th century. By 1821 eighty-five turnpikes had been chartered in the state. A network of paved tolls were develop leading from the city to New York, Reading, Harrisburg, Lancaster, Baltimore, and into New Jersey (Philadelphia:300, 230).

The 1790 National Census

The year 1790, is theoretically foundational in defining the milieu of the Industrial Age. This year included the first national census as established by the ratification of the United States Constitution.

Conceptually, the inception of a census is integral to this project’s argument. Political and economic reasoning notwithstanding, a census may be principally based on the movement of people and intended to study and record occupants, out of the knowledge that people must be immigrating or migrating, conceivably at new, more-often frequencies. In our project, we attempt to chart the networks that develop from these travels, and show how those networks were influenced or changed by the conditions of the Industrial Age. We first come to perceive how movement is symptomatic in a changing generational attitude, as seen by by Thomas Pim Cope’s enterprising move from Lancaster to Philadelphia year 1784, a few years before the landmark year of 1790.
